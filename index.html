Vote Yes

Vote Yes on May 2nd for the Warmth and Safety of Benton Harbor Students

What is on the May 2 ballot?

On May 2, voters in the Benton Harbor Area Schools district will be asked to renew a two-mill sinking fund millage for 10 years. The sinking fund millage will generate approximately $1.3 million each year to keep school facilities in good repair. The sinking fund dollars can only be used for specific purchases set forth by the State of Michigan. Funds cannot be used for employee salaries and benefits.



What is a “sinking fund”?

A sinking fund is separately maintained account into which a local school district can deposit voter approved local millage revenue to fund facility projects or repairs and eligible security and technology costs as they arise rather than borrowing funds for these expenses.

A sinking fund provide districts with a cost-effective alternative to borrowing or bonding for some expenses because the sinking fund does not bring interest costs, bond underwriting costs, or hefty legal fees associated with bond issues.

What is the purpose of the sinking fund?

The goal of the building and site “sinking fund” millage is to maintain our facilities over the next 10 years, and to provide safe, comfortable, and educationally appropriate learning environments for our children.

How does a sinking fund help Benton Harbor Area Schools?

A sinking fund helps relieve the General Fund of expensive facility costs such as roof replacements, boiler replacements, parking lot re-construction, and other “capital expenditures” that would otherwise be paid from the General Fund. Relieving this burden from the General Fund would allow more dollars to be directed specifically to the classroom.

Financial Questions

Will this millage increase my property taxes?

No. The requested millage rate (two mills) is exactly the same as the sinking fund millage rate levied in 2016.

Is this a new millage?

No, the District has had a sinking fund for years.

How much revenue will the sinking fund generate?

At current property values, two mills generates approximately $1.38 million per year for the district. Taxable values and tax collections vary from year to year, so the actual revenue collected will vary.

What is a mill?

A mill represents $1 for every $1,000 of taxable value of your property. The taxable value of your property is the same, or lower than, the state equalized value (SEV). The taxable value is approximately 50% (but often less than 50%) of the market value of your property.

How many mills will be levied?

Two mills, exactly the same as the sinking fund millage rate that was levied in 2015.

Permissable Uses of
Sinking Fund Proceeds

How can a sinking fund be used?

Sinking Funds can be used for remodeling, repair/replacement of buildings and sites. The funds can also be used for eligible security and technology costs.

Expenses must be audited for compliance with the Michigan Department of Treasury guidelines.

Can the money from the sinking fund be used to pay employee salaries, wages and benefits?

No. The sinking fund is a specific and restricted fund that can only be used for legally permissible purposes such as the construction, renovation and repair of schools and other facilities.

School districts are prohibited from using sinking fund revenue to pay for operating expenses such as teacher, administrator or employee salaries or benefits, textbooks, and more.

Are school district permitted to commingle sinking fund revenue with other funds?

No, sinking funds must be kept separate from the district’s operating funds

Will the sinking fund be used to purchase land?

No, the sinking fund will not be used for the purchase of land or other property. Michigan law permits sinking funds to be used to purchase land, but the District removed this language from the ballot since there are no plans to purchase land in the foreseeable future.

How can taxpayers be assured that the sinking fund proceeds are being used appropriately?

A school district that levies a sinking fund tax is required to receive an independent audit of its sinking fund payments each year, including a review of the uses of the sinking fund, and is required to submit the audit report to the Department of Treasury.

If the Department of Treasury determines from the audit report that the sinking fund has been used for a purpose other than those authorized for the sinking fund, the school district is required to repay the misused funds to the sinking fund from the school district's operating funds and is not allowed to levy a sinking fund tax after the date the Department of Treasury makes that determination.

Ballot Wording

How will the sinking fund millage proposal appear on the ballot?

SINKING FUND MILLAGE PROPOSAL


Shall the limitation on the amount of taxes which may be assessed against all property in Benton Harbor Area Schools, Berrien County, Michigan, be increased by and the board of education be authorized to levy not to exceed 2 mills ($2.00 on each $1,000 of taxable valuation) for a period of 10 years, 2017 to 2026, inclusive, to create a sinking fund for construction or repair of school buildings, for school security improvements, for the acquisition or upgrading of technology and all other purposes authorized by law, the estimate of the revenue the school district will collect if the millage is approved as levied in 2017 is approximately $1,380,400.

Why does the ballot call this millage an “increase” instead of a “renewal”?

A newly enacted Michigan law added certain security and technology costs to the types of costs than school districts can legally spend sinking fund dollars on. Even though the millage rate for the proposed millage will remain exactly the same (2 mills), the additional permissible uses, security and technology, necessitated the word “increase” instead of “renewal.”

Will the energy efficiency of our buildings be improved with these projects?

Yes, some of the projects are expected to improve energy efficiency and reduce utility costs. This may include replacement of one or more boilers, replacing energy inefficient lighting, and more.

How does the millage rate for Benton Harbor Area Schools compare to those of other school districts in Berrien County?

Of the 13 K-12 public school districts in Berrien County, Benton Harbor Area Schools had one of the four lowest K-12 school millage rates in 2015, the most recent year for which comparative data is available on the Michigan Department of Education website. Benton Harbor’s homestead millage rate, again from 2015, was 43% lower than the average for all K-12 public school districts in Berrien County.

Do other districts in Berrien County have a sinking fund, too?

In 2015, eight K-12 public school districts in Berrien County had a sinking fund.

How will the proposed sinking fund millage benefit students and the community?

The money raised through continuation of the two-mill sinking fund millage will help:
  • Provide a safe, secure and healthy environment for the students of Benton Harbor Area Schools
  • Ensure that the limited operating funds are available to support student learning
  • Protect the community’s financial investment in Benton Harbor Area Schools’ buildings and facilities
  • Allow for continued progress on the district’s deficit elimination plan

What are the benefits of the Building and Site Sinking Fund proposal for residents who don’t have students in the district?

Updated schools can maintain or enhance the property values of our residents.

Can building and site sinking fund dollars be used for building security measures?

Yes, for example, sinking fund dollars can be used for building entrance modifications, keyless entry systems, and video surveillance infrastructure.
